Grief After Addiction

Shared by: Anonymous

Addiction is hard, not only for the addict, but for their family as well. I'm wanting to share this story solely because I don't want anyone to feel alone on this topic.

My story is based on a daughter of an addict. I am that daughter. I'm sure there are many who have also had a similar experience.

My father was such a funny and social man and his singing sounded like a voice of an angel. If you met him, you always remembered him. His laugh was contagious, his jokes were hilarious, and long conversations with him never felt tedious. You could never tell beneath the smile and humor that depression lingered and grew unbearable over time.

He was a pastor. He was a husband. He was a father. Mental health had always been something he struggled with, but he also faced serious metabolic health issues.

In 2002, medical technology wasn't what it is today. My family was told that without bariatric surgery, he likely wouldn't live long enough to see my brother and me reach our milestones. He feared becoming addicted to pain medication because of his father's history, but his love for his children outweighed that fear.

The surgery revealed an inherited blood disorder called Mediterranean Hemolytic Anemia. Because of complications, he lived in constant pain and was prescribed narcotics. I remember, even as a toddler, seeing him constantly sick. I didn't understand addiction. I only saw my dad hurting, so I rubbed his back, brought him water, and unknowingly became one of his caregivers.

Eventually the medication wasn't enough. Alcohol entered the picture. His personality changed. Anger replaced laughter, and abuse entered our home.

One night I woke up holding the teddy bear he had given me and witnessed something no child should ever see. My father threw my mother down the stairs. When he realized I had seen it, he immediately broke down crying. I still remember the flashing police lights through the window and hearing him scream that he loved me.

Eventually he became sober. My parents divorced but remained friends and successfully co-parented. I loved spending time with my dad and believed life had finally turned around.

Years later he remarried. I adored his new wife, but behind closed doors things were not what they appeared. He relapsed, and once again I found myself taking care of him. I brought him washcloths, medicine, even beer, never realizing that a child should never have carried that responsibility.

Then came the day that changed everything.

I was checked out of school and taken to the hospital. My father was lying there bruised, pale, and connected to machines. Before I arrived, he was gone.

I asked if I could hold his hand. My mother said yes. I remember asking why it was so cold. I remember crying into that hand and begging for him to come back.

He had been murdered in a bar.

As I grew older, I realized something heartbreaking. Even though my father loved me deeply and I loved him just as much, I should never have been responsible for taking care of an adult battling addiction.

For years I blamed myself. I wondered if handing him a beer somehow made me responsible.

Sometimes, even now at twenty-five years old, I still catch myself wondering if maybe he never really died. Maybe he's somewhere watching over me from a distance.

People often say time heals. I'm not sure it does.

I still cry. I still feel angry. I still feel confused. What hurts even more is knowing that no one has ever been held accountable for his murder. My family never received justice.

Grief is more than tears. It changes families. It changes relationships. It changes who you become.

My dad was my rock. He was the first man who ever loved me. He was my best friend.

Losing a parent too soon isn't fair. Addiction isn't fair.

The hurt doesn't disappear. You simply learn how to carry it.

I still wonder if he sees my milestones. I hope he does.
